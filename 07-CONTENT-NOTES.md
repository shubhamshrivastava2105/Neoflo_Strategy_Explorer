# Content Notes — What to keep, soften, or remove before going public

The content was written for an internal-first audience. Some passages are
sharper than they need to be for a fully public link. Decide per item below.

---

## Keep as-is (it's already fine for any audience)

- All three explainer pages (P2P, O2C, R2R). These are descriptive, factual,
  and well-suited to any reader.
- The TL;DR of the strategy page. Strong opinion is the point.
- The market sizing section. Standard analyst data, public sources.
- The competitive landscape tables. Everything is public information about
  these vendors.
- "Where the gap is" — the 2x2 grid is observational, not accusatory.
- The "Why this matters for Neoflo" sections of each explainer. Positions
  Neoflo without being aggressive.

---

## Consider softening if going fully public

These are sharper than they need to be for an unknown audience:

### 1. "Peakflo is the competitor" passages

**Internal version (current):**
> "The blunt assessment: if we don't have a sharper differentiation story
> vs Peakflo within 90 days, we will lose deals to them in Singapore."

**Softer public version:**
> "Peakflo is the most directly comparable platform in the SEA mid-market
> segment. A clear differentiation story is essential."

**Recommendation:** Keep the sharper version if the link audience is internal
+ trusted prospects. Soften only if you're going to LinkedIn-public with the
URL.

### 2. The Risks section, specifically Risk 1 (Peakflo accelerates)

The current framing is direct. It's fine for an internal artifact and even
for sophisticated investors. It's slightly off-tone for a fully public web
artifact — naming a competitor in a "risks" section reads as defensive.

**Option A (keep):** Leave it. It demonstrates clear-eyed thinking.
**Option B (soften):** Reframe as "competitive dynamics to monitor" instead
of "risks." Same content, less defensive register.

### 3. "We have no US sales motion" / "5–10x CAC"

This is honest internal language but reads as Neoflo admitting weakness in
public. For a public artifact:

**Softer version:** "Our distribution and reference advantage in Southeast
Asia is structurally stronger than in the US, where multiple incumbents
have 3–7 year head starts."

Same idea, different register. Less self-deprecating.

### 4. The "What would change my mind" section

This is **excellent** for an internal audience and senior peers — it
demonstrates intellectual honesty. For a totally cold public audience it
might read as wavering.

**Recommendation:** Keep it. Anyone discerning enough to be on this link
will appreciate the rigor. It's also a good filter — readers who don't
like nuance aren't your target.

## Things to add before going public

### 1. A "Who wrote this and why" section

Something like a `/about` page or footer expansion:

> "I'm Shubham Shrivastava, Head of Product at Neoflo. This is a strategic
> artifact I built to make my own thinking visible — both to my team and
> to anyone outside who's curious about FinOps. If anything here resonates
> or you disagree, I'd genuinely like to hear why: shubham.s@neoflo.ai"

This humanizes the artifact and converts readers into conversations.

### 2. "How this was made" footnote (optional but distinctive)

> "Built with Claude Code in [X] hours. Content authored by me, prototype
> code authored by Claude. The whole thing runs as a Next.js app on
> Vercel."

This is a meta-statement that some audiences (founders, builders, AI
practitioners) will love. Skip it if your target reader is a CFO who
doesn't want to know how the sausage is made.

### 3. A Contact / "I'd like to talk" pathway

Right now the artifact has no conversion mechanism. That's intentional —
it's not a marketing site. But for prospects who DO want to talk after
reading, there should be at least an email link in the footer.

**Minimum:** "shubham.s@neoflo.ai" in the footer.
**Better:** A small `/talk` page with a paragraph + email + LinkedIn link.

## Things to deliberately leave out

- Pricing for Neoflo specifically (we haven't published it; don't anchor)
- Customer names beyond Zalora (which is already in the existing memo)
- ARR / revenue figures
- Funding details
- Team size / hiring plans
- Roadmap dates more specific than quarters

---

## A note on the link's portability

Once you share the URL with one person, assume it can reach anyone. That
includes:
- Peakflo's team (they will see the "Peakflo is the competitor" framing)
- Other competitors named in the cohorts
- Anyone you didn't mean to share it with

This is fine. The competitive memo is competently argued, not slanderous,
and naming competitors is normal in serious strategy work. But if any
specific phrasing would embarrass you in front of the named party, soften
it before publishing.
