What FinOps is — and why it matters.

FinOps (finance operations) is how a company runs the money-in / money-out / close-the-books machinery: P2P (paying suppliers), O2C (collecting from customers), and R2R (closing and reporting). Everything else on this page refers back to these terms — so start here, then read on.

Why it matters: when these run badly, real money leaks — suppliers get paid late (or twice), customers take 70+ days to pay and cash dries up, and the month-end close drags for weeks or fails an audit. Good FinOps means cash is collected faster, the books close in days, and every payment has an audit trail. That's the pain Neoflo exists to remove.

Note: we use FinOps to mean finance operations (AP / AR / close) — not the cloud-cost-management discipline of the same name.

VAT & WHT — a plain-English guide.

A non-finance walkthrough of two taxes the team will keep running into across P2P and O2C — what they are, when they apply, and how they flow through an invoice. Worked example uses GST (India's VAT) at 12% to make the math concrete.

What is VAT?

VAT stands for Value Added Tax. Think of it as a tax that gets added to the price of a product or service every time it changes hands — from manufacturer to business to end consumer.

The important thing to understand: VAT follows the product, not the person. Every line on an invoice can have its own VAT rate — a luxury item might be taxed at 20%, a basic food item at 5%, some essential items at 0%. It depends on what is being sold, not who is selling it.

What is WHT?

WHT stands for Withholding Tax. Unlike VAT, this has nothing to do with the product — it's entirely about who you're paying. When a company pays a vendor (especially for services), the government's instruction is:

"Before you pay them, hold back a small percentage and send it directly to us."

So instead of the vendor paying their own taxes later, the buyer takes a cut on the government's behalf at the time of payment. WHT follows the vendor, not the product.

The key difference at a glance

VATWHT
What triggers it?The product / service being soldThe vendor being paid
Calculated on?Line items on the invoiceTotal amount payable to vendor
Who collects it?Seller (from buyer)Buyer (deducted before paying vendor)
Who gets the money?Government (via seller)Government (via buyer)

How VAT actually works — the Myntra example

Three players: UrbanThreads (clothing brand selling T-shirts to Myntra), Myntra (the marketplace), You (the end customer). GST rate = 12%.

Step 1 · UrbanThreads sells to Myntra for ₹100

Base price₹100
GST (12%)₹12
Myntra pays UrbanThreads₹112

UrbanThreads now holds ₹12 of tax money — that belongs to the government. But UrbanThreads also paid ₹6 GST on raw materials, so they tell the government: "I collected ₹12 but already paid ₹6 — I'm sending the difference."

✅ UrbanThreads pays government: ₹6

Step 2 · Myntra sells to You for ₹200

Base price₹200
GST (12%)₹24
You pay Myntra₹224

Myntra collected ₹24 but already paid ₹12 in GST to UrbanThreads, so they only owe the difference.

✅ Myntra pays government: ₹12

Step 3 · You — the end customer

You pay ₹224 and go home. You can't claim anything back. The tax stops with you.

✅ You pay: ₹24 in GST. Full stop.

Where did the government's money come from?

WhoPaid to govtWhy
UrbanThreads' raw-material supplier₹6GST on materials sold to UrbanThreads
UrbanThreads₹6Collected ₹12, claimed back ₹6
Myntra₹12Collected ₹24, claimed back ₹12
Total₹24Exactly 12% of the final ₹200

The government collected ₹24 — exactly 12% of the final price. It came in pieces throughout the chain, but it adds up perfectly. That's why it's called "Value Added" tax — each business only pays tax on the value they added (UrbanThreads added ₹50, Myntra added ₹100, you added ₹0 and bear the full tax).

Now where does WHT fit in?

WHT has nothing to do with the customer — it's purely a Myntra ↔ UrbanThreads thing. When Myntra pays UrbanThreads ₹112 (base + GST):

  • WHT applies on the base amount (₹100) at, say, 2%
  • Myntra holds back ₹2
  • UrbanThreads receives ₹110 instead of ₹112
  • Myntra sends that ₹2 directly to the government

UrbanThreads isn't losing the ₹2 forever — it gets credited against their annual income-tax return. It's just prepaid tax, collected early through Myntra. The government's logic: "You're a big, registered company. Make sure the vendor pays their income tax. You hold it for us."

What changes when the vendor is an individual?

Replace UrbanThreads with Ravi, a freelance T-shirt maker. Two scenarios depending on whether Ravi is GST-registered.

Scenario 1 · Ravi is GST-registered

Works almost exactly like UrbanThreads — Ravi charges GST, collects it, files returns, claims input credit on his materials. Myntra deducts WHT before paying him. Everything flows the same way. The only difference is Ravi is a one-man show doing it all himself.

Scenario 2 · Ravi is NOT GST-registered

Below the threshold (~₹20 lakhs/year in India), small vendors aren't required to register for GST — and if they're not registered, they cannot charge GST at all.

  • Ravi sells T-shirts to Myntra for ₹100 — no GST on top
  • Myntra pays him flat ₹100 (no GST collected, no GST forwarded)

Myntra's total outflow ends up the same either way — the difference is timing and paperwork. The real loser is Ravi himself, in two ways:

1. No input credit on his purchases. Ravi paid GST when he bought fabric — but as an unregistered vendor, he can't claim it back. Real cost out of his margin.

2. Higher WHT deduction. Government distrust of less-formal vendors maps directly to a higher withholding rate:

Vendor typeWHT rateWhy
Registered company~2%Files taxes properly, government trusts them
Individual / freelancer~10%Government isn't sure they'll file on their own
Foreign vendor~15–20%No jurisdiction; grab tax before money leaves the country

When Myntra pays Ravi ₹100 → holds back ₹10 (10% WHT) → Ravi receives ₹90, govt gets ₹10. The ₹10 still adjusts at year-end on his income tax — but he gets less cash in hand immediately.

Key takeaways

  1. VAT / GST is about the product. Every line item on an invoice can have a different rate. The tax flows through the chain and the end customer bears it all.
  2. WHT is about the vendor. The more "informal" or "less trusted" the vendor (individual, unregistered, foreign), the higher the WHT rate.
  3. Registered businesses don't really lose money on VAT. They collect it, claim back what they paid, and pass the net amount to the government — they're a pipe the tax flows through.
  4. The real burden falls on unregistered individuals. No input credit on their purchases AND a higher WHT deduction.
  5. Both taxes ultimately protect the government's tax collection — VAT ensures tax is collected at every stage of sale; WHT ensures income tax is collected before money reaches vendors who might not file on their own.

Written for general understanding. Tax rates and rules vary by country and business type — always consult a tax professional for specific advice.

The cross-workflow moat — what none of them have

Every player above runs a process-specific data model: Stampli is invoice-centric, HighRadius is AR-centric, BlackLine is GL-centric. The only other multi-process player is Esker (P2P + O2C) — and even Esker has no R2R.

Neoflo's moat is the seam they can't cross: P2P + O2C + R2R on one engine, against one vendor master, one customer master, and one immutable audit trail. A customer running all three on Neoflo has integrated data across the entire cash cycle — a switching cost a single-workflow vendor cannot replicate without rebuilding their data layer.

Where it's still being built: reference depth (early book), the compounding data/extraction loop (real but young), and R2R coverage (the third module). The defensible position is "first to all three on one data layer" — the window is real but not permanent.

Targeting · Boundaries

Disqualifiers — when we say no.

Consolidated rules for "we don't pursue this." If a deal trips one of these, walk — politely. Time saved here funds the deals we're built to win.

Hard disqualifiers (walk immediately)

  • "Our CTO wants to build it in-house." Per our ICP definition this is a deal-killer; CTO budget control means cycles burned for no win. Walk; revisit if dynamic changes.
  • India domestic mid-market AP. Sub-$5 / invoice OCR commodity (Cogniquest, Nanonets, Kosh.ai, ClearTax) destroys SaaS unit economics. Flipkart loss to Uttara at ₹15 / RO is the canonical proof. Decline; refer to commodity vendors.
  • US tech / SaaS / DTC scale-ups. Brex / Ramp own the segment via card distribution; cold outbound is dead per Pattern 1; CAC would be 5–10× SEA. Refer to Stampli / Bill / Vic.ai if asked.
  • EU mid-market (until 2028 reassessment). No warm channel; GDPR is a heavy lift; regional incumbents (Yooz, Esker, Medius, Sidetrade) are entrenched.

Yellow flags (qualify carefully, may still close)

  • Single-product ask only. Hitachi walked because we were AP-only when they wanted cash app. Re-frame to multi-product platform; if buyer insists single-product, scope a pilot but flag platform expansion as the goal.
  • On-prem hard requirement. Welspun-style. Possible but heavy delivery; only with scope and price reflecting it.
  • "Free" alternative dominant. Cards-bundled bill-pay (Brex / Ramp) or ERP-native AP (SAP VIM, Oracle Fusion AP). Win only if the buyer has explicitly named pain with the incumbent.
  • No multi-entity, no multi-currency. Workflow-engine generality is our moat; if neither applies, we have less differentiation.
  • Sub-$25K ARR realistic deal size. Below the floor for our gross-margin profile. Refer to Bill.com / Stampli SMB tier if the customer fits there.

Geography rules (pre-cleared yes / no)

  • Yes — primary: US mid-market — SAP-shop enterprise (via warm CPA partners: Rehmann, KPMG alumni) and the broader US mid-market
  • No: India domestic; US tech / SaaS scale-ups; EU mid-market
